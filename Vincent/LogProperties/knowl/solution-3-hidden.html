1. We apply the Change of Base formula with \(a=3\) and \(b=10\) to obtain \(3^2 = 10^{2 \log(3)}\text{.}\) Typing the latter in the calculator produces an answer of \(9\) as required.

  2. Here, \(a=2\) and \(b = e\) so we have \(2^{x} = e^{x \ln(2)}\text{.}\) To verify this on our calculator, we can graph \(f(x) = 2^x\) (in red) and \(g(x) = e^{x \ln(2)}\) (in blue). Their graphs are indistinguishable which provides evidence that they are the same function: see Figure 1.2.

  3. Applying the change of base with \(a=4\) and \(b=e\) leads us to write \(\log_{4}(5) = \frac{\ln(5)}{\ln(4)}\text{.}\) Evaluating this in the calculator gives \(\frac{\ln(5)}{\ln(4)} \approx 1.16\text{.}\) How do we check this really is the value of \(\log_{4}(5)\text{?}\) By definition, \(\log_{4}(5)\) is the exponent we put on \(4\) to get \(5\text{.}\) The plot from GeoGebra in Figure 1.3 confirms this. (Which means if it is lying to us about the first answer it gave us, at least it is being consistent.)

  4. We write \(\ln(x) = \log_{e}(x) = \frac{\log(x)}{\log(e)}\text{.}\) We graph both \(f(x) = \ln(x)\) and \(g(x) = \frac{\log(x)}{\log(e)}\) and find both graphs appear to be identical.
