  1. To expand \(\log_{2}\left(\frac{8}{x}\right)\text{,}\) we use the Quotient Rule identifying \(u = 8\) and \(w=x\) and simplify.

    \begin{align*} \log_{2}\left(\dfrac{8}{x}\right) \amp = \log_{2}(8) - \log_{2}(x) \tag{Quotient Rule}\\ \amp = 3 - \log_{2}(x) \tag{Since $2^{3} = 8$}\\ \amp = - \log_{2}(x) + 3 \end{align*}

  2. In the expression \(\log_{0.1} \left(10 x^2 \right)\text{,}\) we have a power (the \(x^2\)) and a product. In order to use the Product Rule, the entire quantity inside the logarithm must be raised to the same exponent. Since the exponent \(2\) applies only to the \(x\text{,}\) we first apply the Product Rule with \(u=10\) and \(w=x^2\text{.}\) Once we get the \(x^2\) by itself inside the log, we may apply the Power Rule with \(u=x\) and \(w=2\) and simplify.

    \begin{align*} \log_{0.1} \left(10 x^2 \right) \amp = \log_{0.1} (10) + \log_{0.1} \left(x^2 \right) \tag{Product Rule}\\ \amp = \log_{0.1} (10)+ 2 \log_{0.1} (x) \tag{Power Rule}\\ \amp = -1 + 2 \log_{0.1} (x) \tag{Since $(0.1)^{-1} = 10$}\\ \amp = 2 \log_{0.1} (x) - 1 \end{align*}

  3. We have a power, quotient and product occurring in \(\ln \left(\frac{3}{ex}\right)^2\text{.}\) Since the exponent \(2\) applies to the entire quantity inside the logarithm, we begin with the Power Rule with \(u=\frac{3}{ex}\) and \(w = 2\text{.}\) Next, we see the Quotient Rule is applicable, with \(u=3\) and \(w=ex\text{,}\) so we replace \(\ln\left(\frac{3}{ex}\right)\) with the quantity \(\ln(3) - \ln(ex)\text{.}\) Since \(\ln \left(\frac{3}{ex}\right)\) is being multiplied by \(2\text{,}\) the entire quantity \(\ln(3) - \ln(ex)\) is multiplied by \(2\text{.}\) Finally, we apply the Product Rule with \(u=e\) and \(w=x\text{,}\) and replace \(\ln(ex)\) with the quantity \(\ln(e) + \ln(x)\text{,}\) and simplify, keeping in mind that the natural log is log base \(e\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} \ln \left(\dfrac{3}{ex}\right)^2 \amp = 2 \ln \left(\dfrac{3}{ex}\right) \tag{Power Rule}\\ \amp = 2 \left[ \ln(3) - \ln(ex) \right] \tag{Quotient Rule}\\ \amp = 2 \ln(3) - 2\ln(ex)\\ \amp = 2 \ln(3) - 2\left[\ln(e) + \ln(x)\right] \tag{Product Rule}\\ \amp = 2 \ln(3) - 2\ln(e) - 2 \ln(x)\\ \amp = 2\ln(3) - 2 - 2 \ln(x) \tag{Since $e^{1} = e$}\\ \amp = - 2 \ln(x) + 2\ln(3) - 2 \end{align*}

We begin by using the Power Rule, and we keep in mind that the common log is log base \(10\text{.}\)

    At this point in the text, the reader is encouraged to carefully read through each step and think of which quantity is playing the role of \(u\) and which is playing the role of \(w\) as we apply each property.

    \begin{align*} \log \sqrt[3]{\dfrac{100 x^2}{yz^5}} \amp = \log \left(\dfrac{100 x^2}{yz^5}\right)^{1/3}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} \log\left(\dfrac{100 x^2}{yz^5}\right) \tag{Power Rule}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} \left[ \log\left(100x^2\right) - \log\left(yz^5\right) \right] \tag{Quotient Rule}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3}\log\left(100x^2\right) - \frac{1}{3}\log\left(yz^5\right)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3}\left[ \log(100) + \log\left(x^2\right)\right] - \frac{1}{3} \left[ \log(y) + \log\left(z^5\right) \right] \tag{Product Rule}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} \log(100) + \frac{1}{3} \log\left(x^2\right) - \frac{1}{3} \log(y) - \frac{1}{3} \log\left(z^5\right)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} \log(100) + \frac{2}{3} \log(x) - \frac{1}{3} \log(y) - \frac{5}{3} \log(z) \tag{Power Rule}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} \log(x) - \frac{1}{3} \log(y) - \frac{5}{3} \log(z) \tag{Since $10^2=100$}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{3} \log(x) - \frac{1}{3} \log(y) - \frac{5}{3} \log(z) + \frac{2}{3} \end{align*}

  5. At first it seems as if we have no means of simplifying \(\log_{117}\left(x^2-4\right)\text{,}\) since none of the properties of logs addresses the issue of expanding a difference inside the logarithm. However, we may factor \(x^2 - 4 = (x+2)(x-2)\) thereby introducing a product which gives us license to use the Product Rule.

    \begin{align*} \log_{117}\left(x^2-4\right) \amp = \log_{117} \left[(x+2)(x-2)\right] \tag{Factor}\\ \amp = \log_{117}(x+2) + \log_{117}(x-2) \tag{Product Rule} \end{align*}
