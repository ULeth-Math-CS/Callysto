Most, but not all, of the usual laws of arithmetic hold for addition and multiplication in \({\mathbb Z}_n\text{.}\) For instance, it is not necessarily true that there is a multiplicative inverse. Consider the multiplication table for \({\mathbb Z}_8\) in Figure Figure 3.1.3. Notice that 2, 4, and 6 do not have multiplicative inverses; that is, for \(n = 2\text{,}\) 4, or 6, there is no integer \(k\) such that \(k n \equiv 1 \pmod{ 8}\text{.}\)
